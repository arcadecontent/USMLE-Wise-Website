The real problem

Most "research" for high schoolers looks good on paper and teaches nothing.

Admissions officers have read thousands of applications. They can tell the difference between a student who did real work and one who paid to have their name added to something. The sad part is that most ambitious students never get the real thing, and it usually comes down to three traps.

01

The mentorship gap

Real researchers are too busy to teach a teenager. So kids get handed busywork with no one actually guiding them.

02

The resume-padding trap

Pay-to-publish journals and a week of shadowing look impressive to a parent and transparent to a reviewer.

03

No real skills

They never learn to read a paper, design a study, or write one. So nothing carries into college or the rest of their career.

Why it works

Real research, taught the way scientists actually learn it.

One real project, owned by your child

They join an actual medical study and carry it for months. Real stakes, real roadblocks, real learning.

Weekly live mentorship

A doctor and research scientist guides them on a call every week and critiques the work the way a real lab does.

The whole arc, in order

From reading a paper to judging evidence, designing a study, handling data, and writing it up.

Present and defend every week

They stand up, explain their work, and answer hard questions. Confidence is a side effect.

AI used the right way

They learn to draft and critique with AI, then check it against the evidence. Never blind trust.

A publication to show

The goal is a peer-reviewed paper and real conference abstracts, with your child's name on them.

Sessions are recorded

If a week gets busy with school, nothing is lost. Every session is saved to rewatch.
